Responsive web redesign

Client: U.S. Department of Education (student project)

Summary

In this student project, my team researched, prototyped and tested a responsive web redesign for a U.S. government agency homepage.

Team

The problem

The U.S. Department of Education homepage:

  1. Lacked a clear target audience
  2. Had complex organization and navigation systems
  3. Was visually dense and unappealing

Result: an intimidating and confusing user experience.

Research

The team's first and most difficult challenge: Who is using this website and why?

Hypothesis: People looking for federal financial aid make up the majority of users.

Discovery

The Department's primary mission is to “establish policies on federal financial aid for education, and distributing as well as monitoring those funds.

The largest portion of its budget goes to its Office of Federal Student Aid, which distributes $112 billion in aid each year from a $187 billion budget.

Nearly 84% of college students benefit from financial aid and two-thirds of students apply for federal aid using the federal FAFSA form administered by the Department of Education.

At this point, we felt confident that most people visiting the website would be looking for information about student aid.

Persona

We felt focusing primarily on students, rather than parents or school counselors, would result in the most inclusive experience.

Usability testing insights

To define pain points around the existing website's usability, we conducted one-on-one user interviews over Zoom with five individuals. Clear trends emerged from usability testing.

  • Pro: Testers were able to complete tasks with relative ease.
  • Con:They complained that the website was dense and not easily scannable.

"Overwhelming amounts of type"

Solutions

Based on these results, I decided to move forward with a redesign focused on three objectives:

  1. focused hierarchical content curation
  2. conversational navigation elements that elevate topics of interest
  3. an easily scannable UI designed to express desired brand personality traits of helpfulness and expertise

The goal: To create an easily digestible homepage focused on clearly delivering relevant content to a primary persona in a visually appealing and approachable manner, building the user's likelihood of pursuing higher education.

Prototyping

My first wireframes applied the principle of hierarchy so that Financial Aid was the first item the primary persona — someone seeking financial aid — would encounter.

Testing

Would use site

80% (4 out of 5) of usability testers strongly agree they would use the site to learn how to get financial aid for college. 20% (1) were neutral.

Easy to scan

60% (3 out of 5) of usability testers strongly agree the page was easy to scan. 20% (1) agree and 20% (1) were neutral.

Understood where to click for info

80% (4 out of 5) of usability testers strongly agree they understood where to click to get more information. 20% (1) disagreed.

Additional feedback

"Not sure what is under 'Resources for You' ... is it more fin aid help?"

"Feels like too much space given for agency logo/menu icon/search bar. A lot of real estate for not much information"

"Sensibilities are definitely very modern and this feels like any website that I would encounter in real life"

Iterations

Improved content grouping

I updated the wireframe design to better group related content, pushing all financial aid-related information to the top of the page to better serve my persona.

Tightened mobile spacing

Compressed space for logo/menu/alert banner and moved headline above main image based on mobile usability testing feedback, added “Menu” under hamburger menu icon to improve accessibility, added a more direct call-to-action with “log in” phrasing.

Clarified navigational menu item

Changed “Resources For You” to “Learning Resources" based on mobile usability testing feedback.

A/B image testing

I conducted A/B testing to select a header image and headline style. I incorporated the winning choices when applying my UI visual design elements.

Conclusion and final prototypes

In conclusion, I found delving into the U.S. Department of Education website to be an interesting challenge. Because a government agency has so many potential users, it was hard to decide which was a primary user persona.

​If I could do anything differently, I would prefer to have included a younger adult subset among the usability testers since they would be among the primary user group. ​It also would have been helpful to have website traffic data to help inform which resources have traditionally been more popular among users.

Overall, I’m pleased that usability testing of my desktop and mobile prototypes indicated that I was successful in keeping the site financial aid task-completion-friendly while meeting the goals of becoming more focused, easier to scan and more visually engaging.
